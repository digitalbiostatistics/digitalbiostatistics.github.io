# Research

## The Ongoing Future

Imagine a person experiencing an <strong>acute coronary syndrome</strong> alone at home, wearing a <strong>device</strong> that continuously monitors <strong>vital signs</strong>. As the device detects critical changes—such as an abnormal electrocardiogram or a drop in oxygen levels—it automatically triggers an <strong>emergency response</strong>. Within seconds, the system locates and dispatches the nearest available ambulance, adapting in <strong>real-time</strong> to factors like traffic conditions and hospital capacity.

While en route, the device continuously transmits vital patient data, enabling dynamic adjustments to <strong>medical</strong> and <strong>logistical</strong> decisions. If the patient's condition worsens, the <strong>hospital</strong> is alerted to prepare a critical care unit. Upon arrival, predictive models guide <strong>decision-making</strong>, ensuring the best possible outcome while maximising <strong>healthcare resource efficiency</strong>.

Currently, existing models struggle with handling large datasets and achieving statistical efficiency due to the absence of mathematical frameworks that seamlessly integrate <strong>statistical modeling</strong> with <strong>real-time decision-making</strong>.

Effective management of <strong>digital health data</strong> at both <strong>individual</strong> and <strong>population levels</strong> requires models that can handle <strong>structured</strong> and <strong>unstructured data</strong> measured at varying frequencies from multiple devices. This complexity poses significant challenges for statistical inference and uncertainty quantification.

Additional technical limitations, such as communication network constraints, device measurement accuracy, and human operational networks, further complicate the integration process. This proposal aims to develop the <strong>mathematical tools</strong> necessary to advance this scenario in the coming years, in alignment with developments in <strong>healthcare infrastructures</strong>.

## Our Mission and Vision

Our long-term goal is to build a healthcare decision-making framework that supports better clinical and public health decisions at both the individual and population levels by combining biostatistics, digital health technologies, and operations research.

We are currently developing statistical and artificial intelligence methods for complex clinical problems involving continuous, high-frequency, and real-time health data. We want these methods not only to answer existing medical questions, but also to help identify the questions that matter most.

What diet is best for a particular person?

How much physical activity does someone need to support healthy aging and longevity?

How should these recommendations change according to an individual’s clinical profile, biology, environment, and lifestyle?

To address these questions, our research brings together causal inference, survival analysis, conformal inference and uncertainty quantification, random-effects and multilevel models, generative AI, spatiotemporal modeling, functional data analysis, and statistical learning for non-Euclidean data and other complex data structures.

Careful study design and computational scalability are essential to this vision. Our goal is to develop methods that are mathematically rigorous, computationally efficient, and practical enough to be used with large-scale biomedical resources such as the UK Biobank, the All of Us Research Program, the Human Phenotype Project, and NHANES.
